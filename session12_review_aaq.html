Sleep Deprivation and Next-Day Memory Performance in College Students
Research article for analysis
Introduction
Prior research suggests that sleep plays a role in memory consolidation, the process by which the brain organizes and stores information learned during the day. Researchers hypothesized that college students who are experimentally restricted to fewer hours of sleep would perform significantly worse on a next-day memory task than students who sleep a full night.
Participants
Participants were 60 college students (ages 18–22), recruited from an introductory psychology course using random sampling from the full course roster. All participants provided informed consent, and were informed they could withdraw from the study at any time without penalty to their course grade.
Method
Participants were randomly assigned to one of two conditions: a full-sleep group (permitted 8 hours of sleep, monitored via a sleep-tracking wristband) or a sleep-restricted group (limited to 4 hours of sleep, also monitored via wristband). The following morning, all participants completed a standardized word-list memory task, operationally defined as the number of correctly recalled words out of a 30-word list studied the previous evening. Researchers debriefed all participants after data collection, explaining the study's true purpose and offering resources for students concerned about their sleep habits.
Results & Discussion
The full-sleep group recalled significantly more words (M = 21.4, SD = 3.2) than the sleep-restricted group (M = 14.8, SD = 4.1), an independent-samples t-test showing this difference was statistically significant, t(58) = 6.85, p < .001, with a large effect size (Cohen's d = 1.77).

0 / 2 pts
Model answer: The findings support the hypothesis: students who slept a full 8 hours recalled significantly more words than those restricted to 4 hours, a large and statistically significant effect (d = 1.77, p < .001). This connects directly to memory consolidation theory, which proposes that sleep helps the brain organize and store newly learned information. Since sleep-restricted students recalled fewer words, this suggests that reduced sleep may have interfered with the brain's ability to consolidate the word list into memory overnight.
