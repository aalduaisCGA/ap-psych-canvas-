Review Evidence-Based Question — Biological and Psychological Influences on Stress

This review EBQ draws on perspectives, nervous system, and brain content from Unit 1, alongside research design and statistics skills from Unit 0. Read all three sources, then answer Parts A through C(ii).

6 points total 30 min recommended
Live score 0 / 6 points
Claim to evaluate
Individual differences in stress responses are best explained by a single factor, such as genetics alone or environment alone.
Source 1: Genetic Contribution to Stress Reactivity in Twins
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
Researchers examined whether genetics alone could explain differences in how strongly people's sympathetic nervous systems respond to stress, comparing monozygotic and dizygotic twins on a standard stress-reactivity measure.
Participants
Participants were 70 twin pairs (35 monozygotic, 35 dizygotic), aged 19–25, recruited via a twin registry using random sampling of registered volunteer pairs. Informed consent was obtained from all participants.
Method
Stress reactivity was operationally defined as the increase in heart rate (beats per minute) from a resting baseline to peak during a timed mental arithmetic task performed in front of evaluators.
Results & Discussion
The correlation in heart rate reactivity between co-twins was significantly higher for monozygotic twins (r = .64, p < .001) than dizygotic twins (r = .29, p < .05).

Source 2: Early Environmental Stress Exposure and Adult Cortisol Response
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
This study examined whether early childhood environmental stress exposure predicts adult stress hormone response, independent of genetic factors, by comparing adults with documented histories of high versus low childhood adversity.
Participants
Participants were 90 adults (ages 25–40) divided into a high-childhood-adversity group (n = 45) and a low-childhood-adversity group (n = 45) based on documented case records, recruited through community health clinics using stratified sampling to balance the two groups by age and sex.
Method
Adult stress response was operationally defined as the percentage increase in salivary cortisol level from baseline to peak following a standardized public speaking stress task.
Results & Discussion
The high-adversity group showed a significantly larger cortisol increase (M = 58.3%, SD = 12.1) than the low-adversity group (M = 31.7%, SD = 9.8), t(88) = 11.2, p < .001, d = 2.38.

Source 3: Combined Genetic and Environmental Prediction of Stress Disorders
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
This study examined whether a combined model, accounting for both genetic risk and environmental stress exposure together, predicts stress-related disorder diagnosis better than either factor alone.
Participants
Participants were 200 adults recruited from three mental health clinics using consecutive sampling of new patients, with informed consent obtained and confidentiality maintained through coded patient files.
Method
Genetic risk was estimated using family history scores (0–10), and environmental stress exposure was measured using a standardized life-stress questionnaire (0–50). Diagnosis of a stress-related disorder was determined by a licensed clinician using standard diagnostic criteria.
Results & Discussion
A statistical model combining both genetic risk and environmental stress scores predicted diagnosis significantly better than either factor alone, accounting for 47% of the variance in diagnosis outcomes, compared to 18% for genetic risk alone and 22% for environmental stress alone.

Part A — 1 point
State whether the claim ("a single factor, genetics alone or environment alone, best explains individual differences in stress responses") is well supported, partially supported, or not supported when considering all three sources together.
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: The claim is not well supported. Source 1 shows a genetic contribution, and Source 2 shows an environmental contribution, but Source 3 directly shows that a combined genetic-plus-environmental model explains far more variance (47%) than either factor alone (18% and 22%), demonstrating that no single factor is sufficient on its own.
Part B(i) — 1 point
Identify one specific piece of evidence from Source 1 or Source 2 that supports a single-factor explanation (genetic or environmental).
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: In Source 1, monozygotic twins showed a significantly higher correlation in stress reactivity (r = .64) than dizygotic twins (r = .29), supporting a genetic contribution. Alternatively, Source 2 shows the high-childhood-adversity group had a significantly larger cortisol response (58.3%) than the low-adversity group (31.7%), t(88) = 11.2, p < .001, supporting an environmental contribution.
Part B(ii) — 1 point
Name a specific psychological concept and fully explain how the evidence from Part B(i) connects to a single-factor explanation.
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: This connects to the concept of heritability. The much higher correlation between monozygotic twins, who share more genetic material than dizygotic twins, suggests that a portion of stress reactivity is inherited. Because monozygotic and dizygotic twins are typically raised in similarly shared environments, the higher correlation specifically among monozygotic pairs points toward a genetic rather than purely environmental explanation.
Part C(i) — 1 point
Identify one specific piece of evidence from Source 3 that challenges a single-factor explanation.
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: Source 3 shows that a model combining both genetic risk and environmental stress explained 47% of the variance in diagnosis, compared to only 18% for genetic risk alone and 22% for environmental stress alone — showing neither factor alone comes close to the explanatory power of the combined model.
Part C(ii) — 2 points
Name a specific psychological concept and fully explain how the evidence from Part C(i) challenges the single-factor claim.
0 / 2 pts
Model answer: This connects to the concept of the biopsychosocial perspective, which holds that behavior results from a combination of biological, psychological, and social factors together, rather than any single cause. The fact that the combined genetic-and-environmental model explained more than double the variance of either factor alone (47% vs. 18% and 22%) directly challenges the claim that a single factor is sufficient — it shows that genetics and environment interact and jointly contribute to stress-related outcomes, consistent with an interactionist rather than single-cause explanation.
