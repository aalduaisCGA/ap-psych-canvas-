Individual Differences in Auditory Absolute Thresholds: A Twin Study
Research article for analysis
Introduction
People vary noticeably in how quiet a sound they can detect. Researchers hypothesized that this variation in auditory absolute threshold sensitivity is influenced by genetics, and predicted that monozygotic (identical) twins would show more similar auditory thresholds to each other than dizygotic (fraternal) twins.
Participants
Participants were 90 twin pairs (45 monozygotic, 45 dizygotic) aged 20–30, recruited through a national twin registry using random sampling of registered volunteer pairs with no reported hearing conditions. All participants provided informed consent.
Method
Auditory absolute threshold was operationally defined as the quietest tone (measured in decibels) a participant could correctly detect on at least 50% of trials, using a standardized audiometric testing procedure conducted in a soundproof booth. Each twin was tested individually to prevent one twin's performance from influencing the other's.
Results & Discussion
The correlation in absolute threshold scores between co-twins was significantly higher for monozygotic twins (r = .69, p < .001) than for dizygotic twins (r = .31, p < .05).

The researchers concluded that the substantially higher correlation among monozygotic twins supports a genetic contribution to individual differences in auditory sensitivity. However, the authors acknowledge that identical twins may share more similar environments and habits (such as noise exposure or music listening habits) than fraternal twins, which could also explain some of the elevated correlation.

Model answer: The findings support the hypothesis that genetics influences auditory absolute threshold sensitivity, since monozygotic twins showed a much stronger correlation (r = .69) than dizygotic twins (r = .31) — exactly the pattern predicted if genetics plays a role. However, the researchers acknowledged an important limitation: identical twins may share more similar environments and habits, such as noise exposure or music listening habits, than fraternal twins. This means some of the higher correlation among monozygotic twins could reflect shared environmental influence rather than genetics alone, making it difficult to fully separate the two explanations.
