Claim to evaluate
Using heuristics always leads to worse decisions than using systematic, algorithmic thinking.
Source 1: The Availability Heuristic and Perceived Risk
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
Researchers examined whether media coverage of rare but vivid events distorts public risk perception through the availability heuristic, comparing perceived risk to actual statistical risk for two causes of death.
Participants
Participants were 150 adults recruited via an online survey platform using random sampling from a national panel. Informed consent was obtained from all participants before beginning the survey.
Method
Participants rated, on a scale of 1 (very unlikely) to 10 (very likely), how likely they believed the average person was to die from a shark attack versus a car accident in a given year. These ratings were then compared to actual national statistical death rates for each cause.
Results & Discussion
Participants rated shark attack risk significantly higher (M = 5.8, SD = 1.4) relative to its actual statistical rarity than car accident risk (M = 4.2, SD = 1.6) relative to its actual statistical frequency, despite car accidents being thousands of times more statistically common, t(149) = 8.9, p < .001.

The researchers concluded that vivid, heavily publicized events like shark attacks are overestimated in likelihood due to the availability heuristic, while far more statistically common risks like car accidents are underestimated by comparison.
Source 2: Heuristic vs Algorithmic Decision Speed and Accuracy Under Time Pressure
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
This study examined whether heuristic-based decisions are always inferior to algorithmic decisions, or whether heuristics may actually perform comparably well under realistic time constraints.
Participants
Participants were 80 emergency room physicians recruited from four hospitals using stratified sampling to balance years of experience across groups. Informed consent was obtained, and confidentiality was maintained using coded participant IDs.
Method
Physicians were given simulated patient cases requiring rapid triage decisions under a strict 60-second time limit (heuristic condition, using quick pattern-recognition judgment) or given the same cases with no time limit and access to a full diagnostic checklist (algorithmic condition). Diagnostic accuracy was operationally defined as the percentage of cases correctly triaged according to expert consensus.
Results & Discussion
Under the 60-second time limit, heuristic-based diagnostic accuracy (M = 87.3%, SD = 6.1) was not significantly different from unlimited-time algorithmic accuracy (M = 89.1%, SD = 5.4), t(78) = 1.42, p = .16, a non-significant difference. However, the algorithmic approach took an average of 14 minutes per case, compared to under 1 minute for the heuristic approach.

The researchers concluded that in time-sensitive real-world contexts, experienced professionals' heuristic judgments can perform nearly as accurately as slower algorithmic methods, challenging the assumption that heuristics are always inferior.
Source 3: Framing Effects in Medical Treatment Decisions
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
Researchers examined whether framing the identical medical statistic in terms of survival rate versus mortality rate would change patients' treatment preferences, even though the underlying facts were mathematically identical.
Participants
Participants were 200 adults recruited from community health clinics using consecutive sampling of patients attending routine appointments. All participants provided informed consent.
Method
Participants were randomly assigned to read a description of a surgical treatment's outcome framed either as "90% survival rate" or the mathematically identical "10% mortality rate," then rated their willingness to choose the surgery on a scale from 1 to 10.
Results & Discussion
Participants given the "90% survival rate" framing rated their willingness to choose surgery significantly higher (M = 7.6, SD = 1.5) than participants given the "10% mortality rate" framing (M = 5.4, SD = 1.8), despite both statements describing identical outcomes, t(198) = 9.3, p < .001, d = 1.31.

The researchers concluded that framing effects can systematically distort decision-making even on identical factual information, a bias that operates regardless of how much time or analytical effort a person applies to the decision.
