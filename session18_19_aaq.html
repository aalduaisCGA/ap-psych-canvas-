The Effect of Elaborative Rehearsal on Long-Term Vocabulary Retention in High School Students
Research article for analysis
Introduction
A large body of memory research distinguishes between shallow encoding strategies, such as simple repetition, and deeper encoding strategies that involve actively connecting new material to existing knowledge or personal meaning. According to the levels of processing model, information that is processed more deeply — for example, by relating a new vocabulary word to a vivid personal example rather than simply repeating its definition — should be retained substantially better over time than information processed only shallowly. Despite widespread anecdotal belief among students that repeated rereading is an effective study strategy, controlled experimental evidence directly comparing rereading (a form of maintenance rehearsal) to elaborative rehearsal in an educationally realistic setting remains limited. The researchers designed this study to directly test whether high school students who use elaborative rehearsal techniques while studying new vocabulary demonstrate significantly better long-term retention than students who use maintenance rehearsal (simple repetition), one week after the initial study session. The researchers hypothesized that students in the elaborative rehearsal condition would significantly outperform students in the maintenance rehearsal condition on a delayed vocabulary recall test administered one week later.
Participants
Participants were 96 tenth-grade students (49 female, 47 male) enrolled in introductory psychology courses across two suburban high schools within the same school district, ranging in age from 15 to 16 years (M = 15.4, SD = 0.5). Students were recruited through their psychology teachers, who distributed informed consent forms to parents and obtained informed assent directly from each student prior to participation. To ensure that neither condition began with a systematic advantage in prior vocabulary knowledge, researchers first administered a brief 10-item pretest covering unrelated vocabulary terms; no significant difference in baseline vocabulary knowledge was found between the two groups that would later be formed, t(94) = 0.87, p = .39. Following this baseline check, students were randomly assigned to one of two conditions: the maintenance rehearsal condition (n = 48) or the elaborative rehearsal condition (n = 48), using a computer-generated random number sequence to assign each student's ID number to a condition.
Method
All participants were given an identical list of 20 unfamiliar vocabulary words along with their definitions, presented one at a time on a computer screen for 15 seconds per word during a single 30-minute study session supervised by a research assistant blind to each student's assigned condition. Students in the maintenance rehearsal condition were instructed to silently repeat each word and its definition to themselves as many times as possible during the 15-second display window, a task designed to encourage shallow, repetition-based encoding. Students in the elaborative rehearsal condition were instead instructed to generate a brief personal example or vivid mental image connecting each word's meaning to their own life experience during the same 15-second window, a task designed to encourage deeper, meaning-based encoding consistent with the levels of processing model. Long-term vocabulary retention was operationally defined as the number of words correctly defined, out of the full 20-word list, on a written free-recall test administered exactly seven days after the initial study session, with no additional study or exposure to the words permitted during the intervening week. All tests were scored by two independent raters blind to condition assignment, using a standardized scoring rubric requiring the core meaning of each definition to be present; inter-rater reliability was high, r = .94.
Results & Discussion
Students in the elaborative rehearsal condition correctly recalled significantly more words one week later (M = 14.8, SD = 2.6) than students in the maintenance rehearsal condition (M = 8.3, SD = 3.1), an independent-samples t-test confirming this difference was statistically significant, t(94) = 11.24, p < .001, with a very large effect size (Cohen's d = 2.29).

The researchers concluded that this large and highly significant difference provides strong support for the levels of processing model, demonstrating that deeper, meaning-based encoding produces substantially better long-term retention than shallow repetition, even when both groups spend identical amounts of time studying identical material. The researchers noted one limitation: because the study was conducted using only tenth-grade students from two schools within a single district, caution should be used before generalizing these findings to other age groups or educational contexts. The researchers also acknowledged that the specific vocabulary list used, while carefully selected to be equally unfamiliar to all participants, may not generalize to all types of academic material, such as complex quantitative or procedural content.

Model answer: The findings strongly support the hypothesis: students using elaborative rehearsal recalled significantly more words (14.8) than students using maintenance rehearsal (8.3), a very large effect (d = 2.29, p < .001). This directly supports the levels of processing model, which proposes that memory strength depends on how deeply information is processed rather than simply how many times it is repeated. Because elaborative rehearsal required students to connect each word to personal meaning (deep, semantic processing), while maintenance rehearsal only required repetition (shallow processing), the large difference in one-week retention demonstrates that depth of processing, not just time spent studying, determines how well information is encoded into long-term memory.
