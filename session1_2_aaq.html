Research Article — Read carefully before writing
SOURCE Does Sleep Duration During Adolescence Predict Academic Achievement and Well-Being? A Longitudinal Study ▼ expand
Introduction
Researchers have long hypothesized that chronic sleep deprivation impairs the cognitive processes necessary for academic achievement — including attention, working memory, and memory consolidation. However, most prior studies relied on self-reported sleep data, which is vulnerable to social desirability bias and inaccuracy. The present longitudinal study aimed to investigate whether objectively measured sleep duration predicts academic performance (GPA) and psychological well-being over a two-year period in high school students. Researchers hypothesized that students who averaged fewer than 7 hours of sleep per night would show significantly lower GPA and well-being scores than students who averaged 8 or more hours.
Participants
Participants were 412 students enrolled in grades 9–11 at two public high schools in the Pacific Northwest. Of these, 51% identified as female, 49% as male. Racial/ethnic composition: 61% White, 14% Hispanic/Latino, 12% Asian, 8% Black/African American, and 5% other/multiracial. Students with a diagnosed sleep disorder were excluded. Parental informed consent and student informed assent were obtained before participation. All participation was voluntary, and students received no academic credit or grade penalty for participation or non-participation.
Method
Students wore actigraphy wristbands (which detect movement patterns to infer sleep/wake cycles) for 21 consecutive school days at the start of each academic year. Based on average nightly sleep duration, students were classified into two naturally occurring groups: Short Sleepers (SS) — fewer than 7 hours per night (n = 178) — and Adequate Sleepers (AS) — 8 or more hours per night (n = 234). Because students were not randomly assigned to these groups (their sleep patterns occurred naturally), this study used a quasi-experimental (ex post facto) design.
Academic performance was operationally defined as the student's official end-of-semester GPA (0.0–4.0 scale), collected from school administrative records at the end of each academic year. Psychological well-being was measured using the validated Warwick-Edinburgh Mental Well-Being Scale (WEMWBS), a 14-item self-report questionnaire with scores ranging from 14 to 70; higher scores indicate greater well-being.
All data were collected at two time points: Year 1 (baseline) and Year 2 (follow-up). Students were debriefed at the conclusion of Year 2 and informed of the study's full purpose.
Results & Discussion
At Year 1, Short Sleepers had significantly lower GPAs (M = 2.68, SD = 0.61) than Adequate Sleepers (M = 3.31, SD = 0.54), t(410) = 11.24, p < .001, d = 1.09. This difference persisted at Year 2 (SS: M = 2.72; AS: M = 3.35), t(410) = 11.78, p < .001.
Short Sleepers also reported significantly lower well-being scores at Year 1 (M = 43.1) than Adequate Sleepers (M = 54.8), p < .001. The GPA difference was consistent across gender and racial/ethnic subgroups within the sample.
The researchers acknowledged a key limitation: because students were not randomly assigned to sleep groups, pre-existing differences (e.g., family stress, screen time habits) may explain part of the GPA difference. Causal conclusions are therefore limited. Nonetheless, the researchers concluded that the data are consistent with the hypothesis that inadequate sleep is associated with lower academic achievement and well-being in adolescents.
