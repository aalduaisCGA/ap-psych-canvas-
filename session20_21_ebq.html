Evidence-Based Question — Is Human Memory a Reliable Record of the Past?

Claim to evaluate
Human memory retrieval functions like an accurate recording device, faithfully preserving past events exactly as they occurred.
Source 1: Leading Questions and the Misinformation Effect in Eyewitness Recall
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
Building on classic eyewitness memory research, this study examined whether subtly different wording in post-event questioning could measurably alter witnesses' later recollection of a staged event, even when the underlying event itself was identical for all participants. The researchers hypothesized that participants asked a leading question implying greater severity of an event would later report more severe and more numerous false details than participants asked a neutrally worded question about the same event.
Participants
Participants were 120 undergraduate students (63 female, 57 male) aged 18 to 24, recruited from an introductory psychology subject pool using random assignment procedures after volunteering for course credit. All participants provided informed consent and were informed they could withdraw at any point without penalty.
Method
All participants viewed an identical eight-second video clip depicting a minor traffic collision between two vehicles at a low speed. Immediately afterward, participants were randomly assigned to answer one of two versions of a follow-up questionnaire: the leading-question group (n = 60) was asked "About how fast were the cars going when they smashed into each other?" while the neutral-question group (n = 60) was asked "About how fast were the cars going when they contacted each other?" One week later, all participants returned and completed a follow-up memory test, operationally defined as their estimated speed in miles per hour and their yes/no response to the question "Did you see any broken glass in the video?" — a detail that did not actually appear in the video for either group.
Results & Discussion
The leading-question group reported a significantly higher average estimated speed (M = 40.8 mph, SD = 6.2) than the neutral-question group (M = 31.4 mph, SD = 5.9), t(118) = 8.42, p < .001, d = 1.54. Additionally, 32% of participants in the leading-question group falsely reported seeing broken glass, compared to only 12% in the neutral-question group, a statistically significant difference, χ²(1) = 7.86, p < .01.

The researchers concluded that subtle differences in question wording alone were sufficient to distort both quantitative estimates and the recall of specific false details, providing strong support for the misinformation effect and raising serious concerns about the reliability of eyewitness memory in legal contexts.
Source 2: Context-Dependent Retrieval and Environmental Reinstatement
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
This study examined whether the accuracy of memory retrieval depends partly on the physical environment present during retrieval, testing whether reinstating the original learning environment could improve recall accuracy for previously learned material, consistent with the concept of context-dependent memory.
Participants
Participants were 80 adults (ages 20–45, M = 28.6, SD = 5.9) recruited from a community volunteer pool using stratified sampling to balance age ranges across groups. All participants provided informed consent, and confidentiality was maintained using coded participant identifiers.
Method
All participants studied an identical list of 30 unrelated words in a distinctively decorated study room featuring specific background music and scent diffusers. One week later, participants were randomly assigned to complete a free-recall test either in the same distinctively decorated room with the same music and scent (matched-context condition, n = 40) or in a plain, unadorned room with no music or scent (mismatched-context condition, n = 40). Recall accuracy was operationally defined as the number of words correctly recalled out of the 30-word list.
Results & Discussion
Participants in the matched-context condition recalled significantly more words (M = 19.6, SD = 3.4) than participants in the mismatched-context condition (M = 13.2, SD = 3.9), t(78) = 7.85, p < .001, d = 1.76.

Source 3: A Systematic Review of Retrieval Practice and Learning Outcomes
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
Retrieval practice — the act of actively recalling information rather than passively reviewing it — has become an increasingly popular focus of teaching and learning research. This systematic literature review was designed to provide an empirically grounded overview of the state of research on whether retrieval practice reliably improves the accuracy and durability of learned information compared to passive study methods.
Participants
Rather than testing individual participants directly, this review analyzed data drawn from 37 classroom-based experimental studies meeting strict selection criteria, representing a combined total of over 5,000 individual student participants across multiple education levels and content areas. Studies were selected using a systematic screening process that reviewed over 2,000 initial abstracts before applying narrow inclusion criteria requiring in-person, individual-student interventions in actual classrooms.
Method
The researchers combined results from 50 separate experiments across the 37 selected studies, calculating 49 individual effect sizes representing the impact of retrieval practice (such as low-stakes practice quizzes) compared to passive review methods (such as simply re-reading material) on subsequent test performance, operationally defined as accuracy on a later summative assessment covering the same material.
Results & Discussion
Statistical analysis of the combined 49 effect sizes showed a consistent, positive effect of retrieval practice on learning outcomes across a wide variety of education levels, content areas, and course designs. The researchers concluded that deliberately incorporating retrieval practice into study routines reliably increases the accuracy and durability of what students are able to later recall, though they noted that very few of the included studies drew participants from non-Western, non-industrialized populations, limiting how broadly the findings can be generalized.
