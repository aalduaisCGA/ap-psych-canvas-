Fluid Intelligence and Susceptibility to the Misinformation Effect in Young Adults
Research article for analysis
Introduction
Prior eyewitness memory research has firmly established that exposure to misleading post-event information can distort a person's memory of an originally witnessed event, a phenomenon known as the misinformation effect. However, relatively little research has examined whether individual differences in cognitive ability — specifically fluid intelligence, the capacity to solve novel problems and process new information quickly — predict how vulnerable a person is to this kind of memory distortion. Some theorists have proposed that individuals with higher fluid intelligence may be better able to critically evaluate incoming information and resist integrating false suggestions into their memory, while others argue that fluid intelligence, which reflects processing speed and novel problem-solving rather than skepticism or critical evaluation specifically, may be unrelated to susceptibility to suggestion. The researchers designed this study to directly test whether fluid intelligence scores predict resistance to the misinformation effect, hypothesizing that participants with higher fluid intelligence scores would show significantly less susceptibility to false post-event information than participants with lower fluid intelligence scores.
Participants
Participants were 110 undergraduate students (58 female, 52 male) aged 18 to 25 (M = 20.3, SD = 1.8), recruited from a university subject pool using random sampling from students enrolled in introductory-level courses across multiple academic departments, in order to avoid a sample composed entirely of psychology majors. All participants provided informed consent prior to participation and were informed they could withdraw at any point without penalty to their course credit.
Method
Fluid intelligence was operationally defined and measured using the Raven's Progressive Matrices test, a widely used, culturally neutral assessment of abstract visual reasoning in which participants select a missing piece to complete a logical pattern, with scores ranging from 0 to 60. All participants completed this measure first. Participants then viewed an identical six-minute video depicting a simulated burglary. Immediately afterward, all participants read a written summary of the video that contained three subtly embedded pieces of false information not actually shown in the video (for example, describing a "red car" when the car in the video was actually blue). One week later, participants completed a 15-item recognition memory test about the video, with susceptibility to the misinformation effect operationally defined as the number of false details (out of the three embedded in the written summary) that participants incorrectly reported as having appeared in the original video. Researchers who scored the recognition tests were blind to each participant's fluid intelligence score to prevent scoring bias.
Results & Discussion
A significant negative correlation was found between fluid intelligence scores and the number of false details incorrectly reported, r = -.46, p < .001, indicating that participants with higher fluid intelligence scores incorporated fewer false details into their memory of the video.

The researchers concluded that this moderate-to-strong negative correlation supports the hypothesis that fluid intelligence provides some protective effect against the misinformation effect, though they emphasized that the correlational nature of this specific analysis means causation cannot be firmly established from the correlation alone, and that other unmeasured variables, such as general attentiveness during the original video or motivation to perform well on the memory test, could not be ruled out as alternative explanations for the relationship.

Part F — 2 points
Explain how the research findings support or refute the researchers' hypothesis, and discuss one limitation the researchers themselves acknowledged that complicates this conclusion.
0 / 2 pts
Model answer: The findings support the hypothesis: participants with higher fluid intelligence incorporated significantly fewer false details (1.1) than participants with lower fluid intelligence (2.2), reflected in the moderate-to-strong negative correlation (r = -.46, p < .001). However, the researchers acknowledged an important limitation: because this is a correlational relationship, causation cannot be firmly established. Other unmeasured variables, such as how attentively a participant watched the original video or their motivation to perform well on the later memory test, could also explain the relationship between fluid intelligence and lower false-detail reporting, rather than fluid intelligence itself directly causing resistance to misinformation.
