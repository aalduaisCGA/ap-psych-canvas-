The Effect of Chunking on Working Memory Capacity for Digit Sequences
Research article for analysis
Introduction
Classic research on working memory capacity suggests that people can typically hold approximately seven items, plus or minus two, in short-term memory at once. Chunking, the process of grouping individual pieces of information into larger, meaningful units, has been proposed as a strategy to effectively expand this apparent capacity limit by reducing the number of individual "items" that must be held in mind. Despite chunking's widespread use as a memory technique, few controlled experiments have directly compared recall performance for chunked versus unchunked digit sequences of equal total length within the same testing session. The researchers hypothesized that participants would correctly recall significantly more digits from sequences presented in chunked groups than from equally long sequences presented as unbroken strings of digits.
Participants
Participants were 84 college students (44 female, 40 male) aged 18 to 23, recruited from an introductory psychology course using random sampling from the full course roster. All participants provided informed consent and were informed their performance would not affect their course grade in any way. Participants reported no known memory-related conditions on a brief background questionnaire administered prior to testing.
Method
Each participant completed two trials in a counterbalanced order to control for order effects: one trial presented a 12-digit sequence as an unbroken string (e.g., "483920175634"), and the other trial presented a different but equally long 12-digit sequence pre-divided into four chunks of three digits each, separated by pauses (e.g., "483 920 175 634"). Each sequence was read aloud by a research assistant at a fixed pace of one digit per second. Working memory capacity for each trial was operationally defined as the number of digits correctly recalled in the correct order immediately after each sequence was presented, out of a maximum possible score of 12.
Results & Discussion
Participants correctly recalled significantly more digits in the chunked condition (M = 10.4, SD = 1.3) than in the unchunked condition (M = 6.8, SD = 1.7), a paired-samples t-test confirming this difference was statistically significant, t(83) = 15.62, p < .001, with a very large effect size (Cohen's d = 2.34).

The researchers concluded that chunking substantially improves recall of digit sequences, consistent with the idea that grouping information into meaningful units allows more total information to be held within working memory's limited capacity. The researchers noted one limitation: the specific 3-digit chunk size used in this study may not represent the optimal chunk size for all types of material, and future research should examine whether these findings extend to non-numerical information, such as letters or words.

Model answer: The findings strongly support the hypothesis: participants recalled significantly more digits in the chunked condition (10.4) than the unchunked condition (6.8), a very large effect (d = 2.34, p < .001). This directly supports the concept of chunking, which proposes that grouping individual pieces of information into larger, meaningful units allows more total information to be encoded and held in working memory. Because working memory capacity is limited to a small number of individual items, reducing 12 individual digits down to just 4 meaningful chunks effectively allowed participants to work within that same capacity limit while retaining more total information.
