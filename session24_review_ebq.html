Review Evidence-Based Question — Can Training Reduce Cognitive Biases?

This review EBQ draws on thinking/decision-making, memory, and intelligence content from Unit 2, alongside research design and statistics skills from Unit 0. Read all three sources, then answer Parts A through C(ii).

6 points total 30 min recommended
Live score 0 / 6 points
Claim to evaluate
Brief training programs can permanently eliminate cognitive biases such as the availability heuristic and confirmation bias.
Source 1: Short-Term Effects of Bias-Awareness Training on Heuristic Use
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
Researchers examined whether a brief educational intervention explaining the availability heuristic could reduce participants' tendency to overestimate the likelihood of vivid but statistically rare events immediately after training.
Participants
Participants were 100 adults recruited through an online research platform using random assignment to conditions after screening for basic reading comprehension. Informed consent was obtained from all participants.
Method
Participants in the training group (n = 50) watched a 10-minute video explaining the availability heuristic with concrete examples, while participants in the control group (n = 50) watched an unrelated 10-minute video on an neutral topic. Immediately afterward, all participants rated the perceived likelihood of several rare, vivid risks (such as shark attacks) relative to more common risks (such as car accidents), with bias magnitude operationally defined as the average difference between perceived and actual statistical risk rankings.
Results & Discussion
The training group showed significantly reduced bias magnitude (M = 1.8, SD = 0.9) compared to the control group (M = 3.4, SD = 1.1) immediately after the intervention, t(98) = 7.94, p < .001, d = 1.59. The researchers concluded that brief training can meaningfully reduce availability heuristic bias in the short term.
Source 2: Long-Term Follow-Up on Bias Training Effectiveness
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
This study followed up on short-term bias-training research by testing whether reductions in cognitive bias persist over a longer time period, rather than only being measured immediately after training.
Participants
Participants were the same 100 adults from a prior short-term bias-training study, recontacted six weeks later using their original contact information; 91 of the original 100 participants (91%) completed the follow-up assessment. Informed consent for the follow-up was obtained separately.
Method
Six weeks after the original training or control video, participants completed the same bias magnitude assessment used in the original study, without any additional training or reminder in between.
Results & Discussion
At the six-week follow-up, the difference between the original training group (M = 3.1, SD = 1.2) and control group (M = 3.3, SD = 1.1) was no longer statistically significant, t(89) = 0.84, p = .40.

Source 3: Repeated Practice and Durable Bias Reduction
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
This study examined whether repeated practice sessions, rather than a single training session, could produce more durable reductions in cognitive bias, drawing on the concept that retrieval practice and distributed learning (rather than one-time exposure) generally produce stronger, longer-lasting learning.
Participants
Participants were 60 adults recruited from a community volunteer pool using random assignment to conditions. Informed consent was obtained from all participants.
Method
The repeated-practice group (n = 30) completed four separate 10-minute bias-awareness training sessions spaced one week apart, while the single-session group (n = 30) completed one 40-minute training session covering identical material all at once. Bias magnitude was measured six weeks after each group's final training session.
Results & Discussion
The repeated-practice group showed significantly lower bias magnitude at six-week follow-up (M = 2.0, SD = 0.8) than the single-session group (M = 3.2, SD = 1.0), t(58) = 5.12, p < .001, d = 1.32. The researchers concluded that spacing training over multiple sessions, consistent with the spacing effect, produced more durable bias reduction than a single, longer training session.
Part A — 1 point
State whether the claim ("brief training programs can permanently eliminate cognitive biases") is well supported, partially supported, or not supported when considering all three sources together.
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: The claim is not well supported. Source 1 shows brief training reduces bias immediately, but Source 2 shows this effect fades entirely within six weeks. Source 3 shows that durable reduction is possible, but only through repeated, spaced practice, not a single brief session — directly contradicting the idea that "brief" training alone can "permanently eliminate" bias.

Name a specific psychological concept and fully explain why the evidence from Part B(i) alone is not sufficient to fully support the claim.
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: This connects to the availability heuristic and the concept of measurement timing. Source 1 only measured bias magnitude immediately after training, providing no evidence about whether this reduction would last. The claim specifically requires "permanent" elimination, and a single immediate measurement cannot establish permanence — a longer follow-up period, as provided in Source 2, is necessary to evaluate that specific claim.
Part C(i) — 1 point
Identify one specific piece of evidence from Source 2 or Source 3 that directly challenges the claim.
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: Source 2 shows that at six-week follow-up, the training group (M = 3.1) was no longer significantly different from the control group (M = 3.3), t(89) = 0.84, p = .40 — showing the initial reduction from the single brief training had disappeared entirely.
Part C(ii) — 2 points
Name a specific psychological concept and fully explain how the evidence from Part C(i), combined with Source 3, provides a complete picture of what actually produces durable bias reduction.
0 / 2 pts
Model answer: This connects to the spacing effect, which shows that distributed practice over time produces more durable learning than massed, one-time exposure. Source 2 shows a single brief training session's effects faded completely within six weeks. Source 3 directly tested this by comparing repeated, spaced training sessions to one longer single session, finding that only the spaced, repeated-practice group maintained significantly lower bias magnitude at six weeks (2.0 vs. 3.2). Together, this shows that durable bias reduction is possible, but requires the same encoding principles that improve memory more broadly — namely spaced, repeated practice — rather than a single brief exposure, directly refuting the specific wording of the claim that brief training alone produces permanent change.
