Social Media Use and Self-Esteem in Adolescents: A Correlational Study
Research article for analysis
Introduction
Adolescence is a developmental period marked by heightened sensitivity to peer feedback and social comparison. Social comparison theory proposes that individuals evaluate their own worth by comparing themselves to others, and researchers have increasingly questioned whether the curated, idealized content typical of social media platforms intensifies these comparisons among teenagers. Prior survey-based research has reported inconsistent associations between social media use and psychological well-being, with some studies finding negative associations and others finding none. The researchers hypothesized that greater daily social media use would be associated with lower self-esteem among high school students.
Participants
Participants were 245 high school students (128 female, 117 male) aged 14 to 18 (M = 16.1, SD = 1.2), recruited from three suburban high schools within the same school district. To ensure the sample reflected the district's overall grade-level composition, researchers used stratified sampling, dividing the eligible student population by grade (9th through 12th) and then randomly selecting participants from each grade in proportion to that grade's share of the total student population. Parental informed consent and student informed assent were obtained prior to participation, and all responses were collected anonymously.
Method
Social media use was measured using the Daily Media Use Questionnaire (DMUQ), on which participants self-reported their average daily minutes spent on Instagram, TikTok, and Snapchat combined, logged across the seven days prior to the study session. Self-esteem was measured using the Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale (RSES), a widely used 10-item instrument with total scores ranging from 10 to 40, where higher scores indicate higher self-esteem. Prior research cited by the study authors reported a test-retest reliability of r = .85 for the RSES over a two-week interval. Both measures were completed anonymously in a single classroom sitting, supervised by a research assistant not affiliated with the students' regular teachers. No manipulation of any variable occurred; both social media use and self-esteem were measured as they naturally existed in each participant.
Results & Discussion
A significant negative correlation was found between daily social media use and RSES scores, r = -.34, p < .01, representing a medium effect size by conventional standards. Students who reported higher average daily social media use tended to report lower self-esteem scores, and vice versa.

The authors note that, because no variable was manipulated, the direction of this relationship cannot be determined from these data alone — lower self-esteem could lead teens to use social media more, greater use could lower self-esteem, or an unmeasured third variable (such as sleep quality or in-person social support) could influence both. The authors also acknowledge that self-reported media use may be affected by social desirability bias, and that despite stratified sampling by grade, the sample was limited to three schools within a single suburban district, which may limit how well the findings generalize to other populations.

Part B — 1 point
Describe the sampling method used in this study and explain one strength of this approach for generalizing the findings.
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: The researchers used stratified sampling, dividing the student population by grade level and then randomly selecting participants from each grade in proportion to that grade's share of the population. A strength of this approach is that it ensures the sample's grade composition closely matches the actual district population, which increases confidence that the results can be generalized to students across all four grades, rather than over-representing one grade.

Evaluate the reliability of the Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale (RSES) as used in this study, using a specific piece of evidence from the article.

Model answer: The RSES appears reliable, since prior research cited in the article reported a test-retest reliability of r = .85 over a two-week interval. Because this correlation is strong and close to +1, it suggests the scale produces consistent scores when the same people take it more than once, which supports its reliability as a measurement tool.
Part D — 1 point
Identify one limitation of this study related to the measurement of social media use, and explain how it could affect the results.
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: Social media use was measured through self-report, which may be affected by social desirability bias — participants might underreport how much time they actually spend on social media if they believe high usage will be judged negatively. This could artificially weaken or distort the true relationship between social media use and self-esteem.
Part E — 1 point
Explain the directionality problem as it applies to this specific study's findings.
0 / 1 pt
Model answer: The directionality problem means we cannot tell which variable caused the other. It is possible that spending more time on social media lowers self-esteem, but it is equally possible that having lower self-esteem causes teens to spend more time on social media, perhaps seeking validation or distraction. Because neither variable was manipulated, the data cannot tell us which explanation is correct.
Part F — 2 points
Name one specific third variable that could explain the correlation found in this study, and fully explain how it could independently affect both social media use and self-esteem.
0 / 2 pts
Model answer: Sleep quality could be a third variable explaining this correlation. Poor sleep is known to negatively affect mood and self-perception, which could independently lower self-esteem. At the same time, teens who sleep poorly may stay awake longer at night, during which time they are more likely to browse social media out of boredom or difficulty falling asleep. In this scenario, sleep quality — not social media use itself — would be the true underlying cause of both the higher social media use and the lower self-esteem scores, making the correlation between them spurious rather than causal.
