Claim to evaluate
Debriefing fully removes any harm caused by deception in psychological research.
Source 1: Immediate Distress Reduction Following Debriefing
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
Researchers investigated whether standard debriefing procedures reduce the psychological distress caused by deception-based studies. Prior research suggested that deception, even when mild, can produce measurable anxiety or discomfort in participants during a study, and the researchers hypothesized that a thorough debriefing session immediately following the study would significantly reduce this distress.
Participants
Participants were 96 undergraduate psychology students (61 female, 35 male) aged 18 to 22, recruited via convenience sampling from an introductory psychology course. All participants provided informed consent, were informed that some aspect of the study procedure would involve mild deception, and were told they could withdraw at any time.
Method
Participants completed a rigged group task in which they were falsely told their performance was far below average compared to other participants, a mild form of deception designed to induce brief social comparison distress. Immediately after the task, before debriefing, participants completed the State-Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI-State subscale), a 20-item instrument with scores ranging from 20 to 80, where higher scores indicate greater momentary anxiety. Participants then received a full debriefing explaining the true purpose of the study and were told their actual performance had not been evaluated at all. Ten minutes after debriefing, participants completed the STAI-State subscale a second time.
Results & Discussion
Mean STAI-State scores dropped significantly from immediately after the deception (M = 52.4, SD = 8.1) to ten minutes after debriefing (M = 34.7, SD = 6.3), a paired-samples t-test showing this difference was statistically significant, t(95) = 14.2, p < .001, with a large effect size (Cohen's d = 1.45). The researchers concluded that debriefing produced a rapid and substantial reduction in measured anxiety following mild deception.

Source 2: Belief Perseverance After Debriefing
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
While prior research has shown that debriefing reduces immediate emotional distress, this study examined whether debriefing also fully corrects false beliefs created by deception. The researchers hypothesized that some participants might continue to hold onto the false information even after being told the truth, a phenomenon known as belief perseverance.
Participants
Participants were 140 adults (ages 19–45, M = 27.3, SD = 6.8) recruited from a community research pool using random sampling from a volunteer registry. All participants gave informed consent prior to the study.
Method
Participants were falsely told, through fabricated feedback, that they possessed unusually low social sensitivity based on a personality questionnaire. After completing several follow-up tasks, participants were fully debriefed and told the feedback had been entirely fabricated and randomly assigned, unrelated to their actual questionnaire answers. One week later, participants completed a follow-up survey asking them to rate, on a scale from 1 (not at all) to 7 (very much), how socially sensitive they believed themselves to be.
Results & Discussion
Despite the debriefing, a significant negative correlation was found between the duration of exposure to the false feedback during the study and self-rated social sensitivity one week later, r = -.41, p < .01. Participants who had spent more time believing the false feedback rated themselves as less socially sensitive a full week later, even after being clearly told the feedback was fabricated. The researchers concluded that debriefing reduces but does not always fully eliminate the lingering effects of deception, a pattern consistent with belief perseverance.
Source 3: Age Differences in Debriefing Comprehension
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
This study examined whether debriefing is equally effective across different age groups, given that comprehension of complex verbal explanations may vary developmentally. Researchers hypothesized that younger participants would show weaker correction of false beliefs following debriefing compared to older participants.
Participants
Participants included 60 children (ages 9–11, M = 10.1, SD = 0.7) and 60 adults (ages 25–40, M = 31.4, SD = 4.5), recruited from a local school and a community center respectively, using stratified sampling to ensure equal representation of each age group. Parental consent and child assent were obtained for the younger group; informed consent was obtained from adult participants.
Method
Both groups were told a mild cover story before completing a simple game, then were debriefed using a standardized, age-appropriate script explaining the true purpose of the study. Immediately after debriefing, participants completed a short comprehension check (5 items, scored 0–5) assessing whether they correctly understood that the original cover story had been false.
Results & Discussion
Adults scored significantly higher on the comprehension check (M = 4.7, SD = 0.5) than children (M = 3.2, SD = 1.1), an independent-samples t-test showing this difference was statistically significant, t(118) = 9.8, p < .001, with a large effect size (Cohen's d = 1.79). The researchers concluded that standard debriefing procedures may be substantially less effective at correcting false beliefs in younger participants, raising concerns about relying on a single standardized debriefing approach across all age groups.

Model answer: This connects to the concept of belief perseverance — the tendency for a belief to persist even after the original evidence supporting it has been discredited. In Source 2, participants continued to rate themselves as less socially sensitive a full week after being clearly told the negative feedback was fabricated, and this effect was stronger the longer they had believed the false information. This directly challenges the claim, because it shows debriefing does not always fully undo the psychological impact of deception — some harm (a distorted self-belief) persisted well beyond the debriefing session itself, meaning debriefing addressed immediate distress (as in Source 1) but not necessarily every lasting effect of the deception.
