Claim to evaluate
Specific psychological functions can be reliably and permanently localized to specific, fixed brain structures.
Source 1: Executive Function Deficits Following Frontal Lobe Damage
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
Researchers examined whether damage specifically to the frontal lobes produces measurable deficits in executive functioning, testing the idea that this cognitive ability is localized to a specific brain region. The researchers hypothesized that patients with documented frontal lobe damage would perform significantly worse on a standard executive functioning task than healthy adults without brain injury.
Participants
Participants included 34 adults with documented frontal lobe damage confirmed via MRI (ages 28–61) and 34 age- and education-matched healthy adults with no history of brain injury, recruited from a neurology clinic and a community volunteer pool respectively. All participants gave informed consent.
Method
Executive functioning was operationally defined and measured using the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST), a standardized neuropsychological task scored by the number of perseverative errors made (errors where a participant continues using an incorrect sorting rule after being told it is wrong), with scores ranging from 0 to 64. Higher scores indicate poorer executive functioning. Both groups completed the identical, standardized version of the task under the same testing conditions.
Results & Discussion
The frontal lobe damage group made significantly more perseverative errors (M = 28.4, SD = 7.2) than the healthy control group (M = 9.1, SD = 4.5), an independent-samples t-test showing this difference was statistically significant, t(66) = 13.1, p < .001, with a very large effect size (Cohen's d = 3.14). The researchers concluded that this large, consistent difference strongly supports frontal lobe localization of executive functioning.

Source 2: Age-Related Differences in Functional Recovery After Brain Injury
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
While brain damage often produces predictable deficits, this study examined whether the brain can sometimes reorganize itself and recover lost function, a process called neuroplasticity. Researchers hypothesized that younger patients, whose brains are generally more adaptable, would show greater recovery of function following localized brain injury than older patients with comparable injuries.
Participants
Participants were 50 patients who had experienced a localized brain injury affecting language-related areas, divided into a younger group (ages 6–12, n = 25) and an older adult group (ages 45–65, n = 25), matched as closely as possible on initial injury severity. Participants were recruited from three rehabilitation hospitals using consecutive sampling of eligible patients admitted during the study period.
Method
Language function recovery was operationally defined as the percentage improvement in scores on a standardized language assessment (0–100 scale) from one month post-injury to twelve months post-injury. Functional MRI scans were also conducted at twelve months to observe whether previously uninvolved brain regions showed increased activation during language tasks, suggesting functional reorganization.
Results & Discussion
The younger group showed significantly greater language recovery (M = 61.3% improvement, SD = 14.2) than the older group (M = 22.7% improvement, SD = 11.8), t(48) = 10.4, p < .001, d = 2.98. fMRI scans additionally showed that 19 of the 25 younger patients displayed increased activation in brain regions adjacent to the original injury site during language tasks, a pattern seen in only 4 of the 25 older patients. The researchers concluded that younger brains showed substantially greater capacity for functional reorganization, suggesting that the localization of psychological functions to specific brain regions may not be as fixed or permanent as often assumed, particularly in younger, more plastic brains.
Source 3: Dopamine Levels and Motor Symptom Severity in Parkinson's Disease
Research summary for analysis
Introduction
This study examined whether motor symptoms in Parkinson's disease are better explained by neurotransmitter activity (specifically dopamine levels) rather than damage to any single fixed brain structure, since Parkinson's disease involves a gradual loss of dopamine-producing cells distributed across a brain pathway rather than one isolated brain region.
Participants
Participants were 60 adults diagnosed with Parkinson's disease (ages 55–78), recruited through a movement disorders clinic using consecutive sampling of newly referred patients. All participants provided informed consent, and confidentiality was maintained through coded patient identifiers.
Method
Motor symptom severity was operationally defined using the Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale (UPDRS) motor subsection, scored from 0 to 108, with higher scores indicating more severe motor impairment. Participants were assessed before and after receiving a standardized dose of L-DOPA, a medication that increases dopamine availability in the brain.
Results & Discussion
A significant negative correlation was found between L-DOPA dosage and UPDRS motor scores, r = -.68, p < .001, meaning higher doses were associated with substantially reduced motor symptom severity. The researchers concluded that motor symptoms were more strongly tied to overall dopamine availability throughout a distributed brain pathway than to damage confined to any single, fixed brain structure, complicating a strict localization account of motor control.
